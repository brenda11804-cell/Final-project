LLM Analysis Report

Analyzing responses from 10 LLMs across 20 AI-related questions

Law Tsz Ki 58737971, Peishimo 59447707, Shao Yuk Shan 59299859

Introduction

Research Question: How do AI models perceive their own use and impact? Do they have a positive attitude toward AI adoption, and do different AI systems provide consistent answers?

We carried out this study to investigate AI models' perspectives on AI usage in education. We explore whether AI systems view themselves as beneficial tools and whether consensus exists among different models regarding AI's role, impact, and regulation.

Methodology

We presented 20 carefully designed multiple-choice questions to 10 different large language models (LLMs). The questions cover four main themes:

  • Future AI usage predictions
  • Current AI usage patterns
  • Educational impacts of AI
  • AI accuracy and regulation

And there are three main types of LLMs to answer here:

  • 3 models of Gemma Series (gemma-3-1b-it, gemma-3-4b-it, gemma-3-270m-it)
  • 5 models of Qwen Series (Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-7B-Instructm, Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct-AWQ)
  • 2 models of Yi Series (Yi-1.5-6B-Chat, Yi-1.5-9B-Chat-AWQ)

Questions included topics such as university policies toward AI, future skill requirements, and AI's effect on productivity and academic performance.

Discussion & Analysis

LLMs tend to give similar answers when dealing with predictive questions. For example, in the question 'By 2027, AI usage among university students will be…', nine LLMs agree that AI usage will increase, while only one believes it will decrease. However, the opposite is true for quantitative questions. As noted in the comparative analysis, "inconsistencies in AI-generated outputs can result in divergent interpretations when LLMs are relied upon for scientific purposes" (Álvarez-Martínez et al., 2025, p. 9). When LLMs are required to provide more precise data, their answers are more divergent. For example, in the question 'AI will reduce achievement gaps for non-native English speakers by…', one analyst believes it will reduce the gap by 0–5%, three by 6–15%, two by 16–30%, three by more than 30%, and one does not give a positive answer. The answers provided by LLMs are scattered. The differences between Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct and Yi-1.5-6B-Chat are particularly pronounced. Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct estimates that AI can reduce learning by 0–5%. It only describes which learning tools utilize AI but lacks analysis of the effectiveness of these tools in learning. Yi-1.5-6B-Chat, on the other hand, estimates reductions of 30% or more, providing several reasons such as personalized learning and multimedia content. Therefore, their answers to quantitative questions tend to be less consistent. Therefore, their answers to quantitative questions tend to be less consistent.

Of the 20 questions provided, all were multiple-choice. Most LLMs selected one option, but some did not provide an answer. For example, Gemma-3-1b-it did not select any answer in 5 of the responses. In the question 'Net learning effect of AI for a typical user', it was expected that the LLM would provide a harmful or beneficial answer, but it only analyzed the potential positive and negative impacts of AI on the user based on the answer options. Its answer did not indicate a preference for harmful or beneficial. Other LLMs also failed to answer any of the 20 questions, except for three LLMs (Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct, and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct) who made explicit selections in all questions.

Key Findings

  • High Consensus Areas:

    9/10 models predict increased AI usage by 2027. 7/10 believe students primarily use AI to save time.

  • Answer Preferences:

    Option C was selected most frequently (61 times, 35% of all answers).

  • Policy Agreement:

    Models agree on the need for university-wide AI policies with disclosure requirements.

Key Concerns

  • Inconsistency Issues:

    Some questions show very low consensus among models (as low as 20% agreement).

  • Model Variations:

    Different AI models exhibit distinct answering patterns and preferences.

  • Risk Perception:

    Models show significant disagreement on AI-related risks and mitigation strategies.

Deep Insights

Our analysis reveals that while AI models show strong consensus on broad trends and motivations (increasing adoption, time-saving benefits), they diverge significantly on implementation details, risk assessment, and technical solutions.

This pattern suggests that while the general direction of AI in education is well-understood across different models, the complexities of practical application, risk management, and ethical considerations remain areas of uncertainty and debate even among AI systems themselves.

The consistency in predicting growth but inconsistency in prescribing solutions highlights the need for continued research and human oversight in AI implementation.
